## Overview / T·ªïng Quan

**English:**
Product-Market Fit (PMF) is the degree to which a product satisfies strong market demand. It's the moment when your product becomes a "must-have" rather than a "nice-to-have."

**Marc Andreessen's Definition (2007):**
> "Product-market fit means being in a good market with a product that can satisfy that market."

**Sean Ellis Test (The Gold Standard):**
Ask your users: *"How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?"*

- **‚â•40% answer "Very disappointed"** ‚Üí You have PMF ‚úÖ
- **<40% answer "Very disappointed"** ‚Üí No PMF yet ‚ùå

**Why PMF Matters:**
- Without PMF: Marketing burns cash, users churn, growth is a struggle
- With PMF: Users pull your product into the market, retention is high, word-of-mouth happens naturally

**Real Example - Superhuman:**
- **2017**: 22% said "very disappointed" (No PMF)
- **2018**: Focused on users who loved it, rebuilt for their needs
- **2019**: 58% said "very disappointed" (Achieved PMF)
- **Result**: Grew from $0 to $20M ARR

## Measuring PMF: The Sean Ellis Test / ƒêo L∆∞·ªùng PMF

**English:**

### The PMF Survey (4 Questions)

**Question 1: The PMF Question (Most Important)**
> "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?"
- Very disappointed
- Somewhat disappointed
- Not disappointed (it isn't really that useful)
- N/A - I no longer use [product]

**Scoring:**
- **‚â•40% "Very disappointed"** = PMF achieved ‚úÖ
- **25-40%** = Getting close, needs work üü°
- **<25%** = No PMF yet ‚ùå

**Question 2: User Type**
> "What type of people do you think would most benefit from [product]?"
- Purpose: Identify your core audience

**Question 3: Main Benefit**
> "What is the main benefit you receive from [product]?"
- Purpose: Understand your value proposition

**Question 4: Improvement**
> "How can we improve [product] for you?"
- Purpose: Prioritize feature development

### How to Run the Survey

1. **Who to Survey**: Active users who have used your product at least 2-3 times
2. **Sample Size**: Minimum 40 responses (ideally 100+)
3. **Timing**: Survey after users have experienced core value (e.g., after 1 week of use)
4. **Tool**: Typeform, Google Forms, or in-app survey

### Interpreting Results

**Scenario A: High PMF Score (‚â•40%)**
- ‚úÖ You have PMF!
- Next: Scale through marketing and sales
- Focus: Doubling down on what works

**Scenario B: Medium PMF Score (25-40%)**
- üü° Close but not there yet
- Next: Identify the segment that loves you (analyze Q2 & Q3)
- Focus: Build for that segment, ignore others temporarily

**Scenario C: Low PMF Score (<25%)**
- ‚ùå No PMF yet
- Next: Talk to users, understand why (Q3 & Q4)
- Focus: Pivot or iterate on value proposition

## Alternative PMF Metrics / Ch·ªâ S·ªë PMF Thay Th·∫ø

**English:**

While the Sean Ellis test is the gold standard, here are other signals:

### 1. Retention Cohorts
- **What**: % of users still active after 30, 60, 90 days
- **PMF Signal**: Retention curve flattens (not dropping to zero)
- **Example**: WhatsApp has 70% D30 retention

### 2. Organic Growth Rate
- **What**: Growth from word-of-mouth (not paid)
- **PMF Signal**: >20% month-over-month organic growth
- **Example**: Dropbox grew 3900% in 15 months (mostly referrals)

### 3. Net Promoter Score (NPS)
- **What**: "How likely are you to recommend [product]?" (0-10 scale)
- **PMF Signal**: NPS > 50
- **Formula**: % Promoters (9-10) - % Detractors (0-6)

### 4. Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) Payback
- **What**: Time to recover cost of acquiring customer
- **PMF Signal**: <12 months payback
- **Formula**: CAC / (Monthly Revenue per Customer)

### 5. Engagement Metrics
- **What**: DAU/MAU ratio (Daily Active / Monthly Active)
- **PMF Signal**:
  - Social products: >20%
  - SaaS products: >40%
  - Consumer apps: >60%

### 6. Churn Rate
- **What**: % of users who stop using your product
- **PMF Signal**:
  - Consumer: <5% monthly churn
  - B2B: <2% monthly churn

## The Path to PMF: 5 Stages / Con ƒê∆∞·ªùng ƒê·∫øn PMF

**English:**

### Stage 1: Problem-Solution Fit
**Question**: Do people have the problem you think they have?
**Method**: User interviews, surveys, pain point validation
**Output**: Validated problem hypothesis

### Stage 2: Minimum Viable Product (MVP)
**Question**: Does your solution actually solve the problem?
**Method**: Build simplest version, test with early adopters
**Output**: Working prototype with core value

### Stage 3: Product-Market Fit
**Question**: Do people love your product?
**Method**: Sean Ellis test, retention analysis
**Output**: ‚â•40% "very disappointed", strong retention

### Stage 4: Scale & Growth
**Question**: Can you profitably acquire customers?
**Method**: Marketing experiments, sales process
**Output**: Positive unit economics, repeatable growth

### Stage 5: Market Leadership
**Question**: Can you dominate the market?
**Method**: Network effects, brand building
**Output**: Category leader, high market share

**Most Companies Fail Between Stage 2-3**
- They build something, but it doesn't achieve PMF
- Don't give up on Stage 2 - iterate until you reach Stage 3

## Playbook: Finding PMF with Superhuman's Process / S√°ch H∆∞·ªõng D·∫´n

**English:**

**Case Study: How Superhuman Went from 22% to 58% PMF Score**

### Step 1: Segment Users (Week 1)
1. Run Sean Ellis survey
2. Create 4 segments:
   - **High-expectation customers**: "Very disappointed" + high usage
   - **Developing customers**: Other active users
   - **Detractors**: Not satisfied
   - **Prospects**: Haven't signed up yet

### Step 2: Analyze High-Expectation Customers (Week 2)
- Study Q2: Who are they? (Role, industry, use case)
- Study Q3: What do they love? (Main benefit)
- Find common patterns

**Superhuman's Discovery:**
- High-expectation customers: Executives & VCs
- Main benefit: "Speed" (keyboard shortcuts, fast email)
- Common pattern: They process 100+ emails/day

### Step 3: Double Down (Weeks 3-8)
- Build features for high-expectation customers ONLY
- Ignore requests from other segments temporarily
- Make the product even better for those who already love it

**Superhuman's Actions:**
- Added more keyboard shortcuts
- Improved speed (sub-100ms response time)
- Built email triage features for high-volume users

### Step 4: Expand Thoughtfully (Weeks 9-12)
- Survey again - PMF score should increase
- Identify next adjacent segment
- Repeat process

**Superhuman's Result:**
- PMF score: 22% ‚Üí 58% in 12 months
- Revenue: $0 ‚Üí $20M ARR in 2 years

## Common Mistakes & How to Avoid / Sai L·∫ßm Th∆∞·ªùng G·∫∑p

### Mistake 1: Surveying the Wrong People
**Problem**: Surveying people who haven't used your product or used it once

**Why It's Wrong**: They haven't experienced enough value to know if they'd miss it

**Solution**:
- Only survey users who've used your product ‚â•3 times
- Wait until they've completed a core workflow
- Filter out inactive users from results

### Mistake 2: Pivoting Too Early
**Problem**: Getting 30% PMF score and immediately pivoting

**Why It's Wrong**: You might be close - sometimes 30% ‚Üí 40% is just a few feature improvements away

**Solution**:
- If score is 25-40%, dig deeper into Q2-Q4
- Identify and double down on users who love you
- Only pivot if score is <20% after 6+ months

### Mistake 3: Trying to Please Everyone
**Problem**: Building features for detractors instead of fans

**Why It's Wrong**: Dilutes your product, confuses your positioning

**Solution**:
- Ignore feature requests from detractors (for now)
- Make fans LOVE you even more
- Expand to adjacent segments only after hitting 40%

### Mistake 4: Confusing PMF with Growth
**Problem**: "We're growing fast, we must have PMF!"

**Why It's Wrong**: You can have growth without PMF (paid acquisition), but it's unsustainable

**Solution**:
- Check retention: Are users staying?
- Check organic growth: Is it word-of-mouth or all paid?
- Check NPS: Would users recommend you?

### Mistake 5: Declaring PMF Too Early
**Problem**: Hitting 40% with 50 users and declaring victory

**Why It's Wrong**: Small sample size, not representative

**Solution**:
- Wait for minimum 40 responses (ideally 100+)
- Validate with other metrics (retention, NPS, organic growth)
- Be honest - PMF is a gradient, not a binary

### Mistake 6: Stopping After Achieving PMF
**Problem**: "We hit 40%, we're done!"

**Why It's Wrong**: PMF can be lost, markets change, competition emerges

**Solution**:
- Re-survey quarterly
- Monitor retention continuously
- Keep improving product

## When to Use PMF Framework / Khi N√†o S·ª≠ D·ª•ng

### Use PMF Framework When:
- ‚úÖ You've launched an MVP and have early users
- ‚úÖ You're deciding whether to scale or iterate
- ‚úÖ You're not sure if users truly love your product
- ‚úÖ Retention is unclear or declining

### Don't Use PMF Framework When:
- ‚ùå You haven't launched yet (use Problem-Solution Fit instead)
- ‚ùå You have <20 active users (not enough data)
- ‚ùå You're in enterprise sales with long sales cycles (use different metrics)
