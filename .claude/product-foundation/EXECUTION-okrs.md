OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) is a goal-setting framework used by Google, Intel, LinkedIn, Twitter, and thousands of companies to set ambitious goals and track outcomes.

**OKR Structure:**

**Example - YouTube (2012):**
```

  KR1: Increase daily watch time from 100M to 1B hours
  KR2: Grow number of channels with >10K subscribers from 10K to 100K
```

- **Alignment**: Everyone knows company priorities
- **Focus**: Limit to 3-5 OKRs prevents spreading thin
- **Transparency**: Public OKRs create accountability
- **Ambitious**: "Moonshot" goals drive innovation
- **Measurable**: Objective tracking, not subjective opinions

## The OKR Framework / Khung OKR

**English:**

### Anatomy of Good OKRs

**OBJECTIVE (The "What")**
- Qualitative and inspirational
- Time-bound (quarterly or annually)
- Action-oriented
- Memorable and motivating

**Good Objectives:**
- âœ… "Become the #1 productivity app for remote teams"
- âœ… "Delight customers with lightning-fast support"
- âœ… "Build the most developer-friendly API"

**Bad Objectives:**
- âŒ "Increase revenue" (not inspiring, no context)
- âŒ "Improve product quality" (vague, not memorable)
- âŒ "Launch 5 features" (outputs, not outcomes)

**KEY RESULTS (The "How to Measure")**
- Quantitative and measurable
- Specific number or percentage
- Time-bound (end of quarter/year)
- Ambitious but achievable

**Good Key Results:**
- âœ… "Increase NPS from 30 to 50"
- âœ… "Reduce page load time from 3s to 1s"
- âœ… "Grow DAU from 10K to 50K"
- âœ… "Achieve 95% on-time delivery rate"

**Bad Key Results:**
- âŒ "Improve user experience" (not measurable)
- âŒ "Launch mobile app" (output, not outcome)
- âŒ "Increase users" (no specific number)
- âŒ "Do better than last quarter" (vague)

### OKR Best Practices

**1. Limit to 3-5 OKRs per level**
- Company: 3-5 OKRs
- Team: 3-5 OKRs (aligned to company)
- Individual: 3-5 OKRs (aligned to team)

**2. Each Objective has 2-5 Key Results**
- Too few KRs: Not comprehensive
- Too many KRs: Lack of focus

**3. 70% Achievement is Success**
- If you hit 100%, goals weren't ambitious enough
- 60-70% = Good performance
- 40-60% = Learning opportunity
- <40% = Rethink approach

**4. Mix Committed & Aspirational OKRs**
- **Committed**: Must achieve (90-100% expected)
- **Aspirational**: Stretch goals (60-70% expected)

## OKR Playbook: Step-by-Step / HÆ°á»›ng Dáº«n Tá»«ng BÆ°á»›c

**English:**

### Phase 1: Planning (Weeks 1-2 of Quarter)

**Step 1: Company OKRs (Leadership Team)**
1. Review company strategy and annual goals
2. Identify top 3-5 priorities for quarter
3. Draft Objectives (aspirational, qualitative)
4. Define Key Results (quantitative, measurable)
5. Present to entire company (transparency)

**Step 2: Team OKRs (Team Leads)**
1. Review company OKRs
2. Identify how team contributes
3. Draft team OKRs (aligned but not cascaded)
4. Ensure 60% aligned to company, 40% team-specific
5. Share with other teams (cross-functional alignment)

**Step 3: Individual OKRs (Contributors)**
1. Review company and team OKRs
2. Define personal contribution
3. Draft 3-5 OKRs (mix of team + personal growth)
4. Discuss with manager
5. Finalize and publish

**Timeline:**
- Week 1: Company OKRs finalized
- Week 2: Team OKRs finalized
- Week 3: Individual OKRs finalized

### Phase 2: Execution (Weeks 3-12 of Quarter)

**Step 4: Weekly Check-ins**
- Review progress on Key Results
- Update percentage completion
- Identify blockers
- Adjust initiatives if needed

**Step 5: Monthly Reviews**
- Formal review with team
- Traffic light status: ğŸŸ¢ On track, ğŸŸ¡ At risk, ğŸ”´ Off track
- Discuss learnings
- Decide: Continue, Pivot, or Cancel

**Step 6: Course Correction**
- If <50% by mid-quarter, consider adjusting
- If external priorities change, update OKRs (rare)
- Focus on impact, not just checking boxes

### Phase 3: Grading (Week 13, End of Quarter)

**Step 7: Grade Each Key Result**
- Assign score: 0.0 to 1.0
- 0.0 = No progress
- 0.3 = Made progress, far from target
- 0.5 = Halfway there
- 0.7 = Success (for aspirational OKRs)
- 1.0 = Exceeded (for committed OKRs)

**Step 8: Objective Scoring**
- Average of Key Result scores
- 0.6-0.7 = Good performance
- 0.8-1.0 = Either too easy or exceptional execution

**Step 9: Reflection**
- What worked? What didn't?
- What did we learn?
- What should we do differently next quarter?

### Phase 4: Next Quarter Planning

**Step 10: Carry Forward or Retire**
- Unfinished OKRs: Carry forward? Retire? Deprioritize?
- Successful OKRs: Celebrate! What's next?
- Failed OKRs: Learn from failure, adjust strategy

## OKR Cadences / Chu Ká»³ OKR

**English:**

### Quarterly OKRs (Most Common)
**When**: Every 3 months
**Best For**: Fast-moving companies, startups, tech
**Pros**: Agile, can pivot quickly
**Cons**: Less strategic, more tactical

### Annual OKRs + Quarterly KRs
**When**: Yearly objectives, quarterly key results
**Best For**: Mature companies, longer sales cycles
**Pros**: Strategic consistency
**Cons**: Less flexibility

### Monthly OKRs (Rare)
**When**: Every month
**Best For**: Very early-stage startups
**Pros**: Maximum agility
**Cons**: Too tactical, not strategic

**Recommendation**: Start with quarterly OKRs

## Common Mistakes / Sai Láº§m ThÆ°á»ng Gáº·p

### Mistake 1: Sandbagging (Setting Easy Goals)
**Problem**: Setting goals you know you'll hit 100%
**Why It's Wrong**: No stretch, no innovation
**Solution**:
- Aim for 60-70% achievement
- Make OKRs aspirational
- Create psychological safety (failure is learning)

### Mistake 2: Waterfall Cascading
**Problem**: Every team OKR must map to company OKR
**Why It's Wrong**: Limits team autonomy, stifles innovation
**Solution**:
- 60% aligned to company priorities
- 40% team-specific innovation
- Allow bottom-up OKRs

### Mistake 3: Business as Usual
**Problem**: OKRs are just regular work, no stretch
**Example**: "Ship features on roadmap" (you'd do anyway)
**Solution**:
- OKRs should be extra push beyond BAU
- Separate "BAU" from "OKR work"

### Mistake 4: Too Many OKRs
**Problem**: 10 objectives, 50 key results
**Why It's Wrong**: No focus, everything is priority
**Solution**:
- Maximum 5 objectives
- 2-5 key results each
- If everything is important, nothing is

### Mistake 5: Output-Based KRs
**Problem**: "Launch mobile app", "Hire 5 engineers"
**Why It's Wrong**: Measures activity, not outcomes
**Solution**:
- Focus on impact: "Reach 100K mobile users"
- Focus on value: "Reduce time-to-hire from 60 to 30 days"

### Mistake 6: No Mid-Quarter Adjustments
**Problem**: Set OKRs in stone, never revisit
**Why It's Wrong**: Market changes, priorities shift
**Solution**:
- Weekly check-ins
- Monthly reviews
- Okay to adjust if fundamentally changed

### Mistake 7: Tying OKRs to Compensation
**Problem**: Bonus based on OKR achievement
**Why It's Wrong**: Incentivizes sandbagging
**Solution**:
- Decouple OKRs from performance reviews
- Use OKRs for alignment, not compensation
- Reward effort and learning, not just results
