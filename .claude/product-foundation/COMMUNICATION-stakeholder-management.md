## Overview / Tá»•ng Quan

**English:**
Stakeholder management is the art of identifying, analyzing, and influencing the people who can impact your project's success. As a PM, you have responsibility without authorityâ€”you must get buy-in without direct control.

**Why Stakeholder Management Matters:**
- **Project Success**: 70% of failed projects cite poor stakeholder management (PMI)
- **Speed**: Good stakeholder management accelerates decisions by 3x
- **Resources**: Stakeholders control budget, headcount, and priorities
- **Career**: Your ability to influence stakeholders determines your impact

**The PM's Challenge:**
- Engineering wants technical excellence
- Design wants perfect UX
- Sales wants features yesterday
- Leadership wants business impact
- You want to ship something valuable

**Your job**: Align these competing interests toward a common goal.

**Real Example - Airbnb's "Snow White" Project (2012):**
**Context**: Brian Chesky (CEO) wanted to redesign the entire product
**Stakeholders**:
- Engineering: "This will take 2 years and break everything"
- Design: "Finally, we can do it right!"
- Growth: "Don't touch what's working"
- Sales/CS: "Our customers will be confused"

**Joe Gebbia's (Co-founder) Approach**:
1. **Mapped stakeholders**: Created power/interest grid
2. **Managed each group differently**:
   - Engineering: Phased rollout, A/B tested (reduced risk)
   - Growth: Data-driven (showed +30% conversion in tests)
   - Sales: Early access, training, FAQ docs
   - CEO (high power): Weekly demos, clear milestones
3. **Result**: Launched in 6 months, +25% bookings, became Airbnb's growth inflection point

## Managing Up: Working with Leadership / Quáº£n LÃ½ LÃªn

**English:**

"Managing up" is influencing your manager and leadership effectively.

### The HBR Model (Gabarro & Kotter, 1980)

**1. Understand Your Boss**

**Their Goals**:
- What are they trying to achieve? (OKRs, promotion criteria)
- What does their boss care about?
- What's their reputation risk?

**Their Pressures**:
- What keeps them up at night?
- Where are they getting heat from above?
- What are their constraints?

**Their Style**:
- **Reader** vs **Listener**: Doc vs verbal update?
- **Detail** vs **Big Picture**: Exec summary vs deep-dive?
- **Frequency**: Daily check-ins vs weekly sync?
- **Decision Style**: Data-driven vs intuition?

**Example**:
- **Boss A** (Reader, Big Picture, Weekly): Send Friday email with 3-bullet update
- **Boss B** (Listener, Detail, Daily): Quick 10-min standup every morning

**2. Adapt Your Communication**

**For Analytical Leaders** (Want data):

**3. No Surprises**

**The Golden Rule**: Never let your boss be surprised in a meeting with their boss.

**Bad**:
- VP asks your boss in staff meeting: "How's Project X?"
- Your boss: "Uh... I think it's going well?"
- VP: "You don't know?!"

**Good**:
- You send Friday update: "Project X is 2 weeks delayed due to API issue. Mitigation plan: switching vendors."
- Your boss in staff meeting: "Project X is on track. We had an API delay but switching vendors, still hitting launch date."
- VP: "Great, thanks for staying on top of it."

**4. Bring Solutions, Not Just Problems**

**Bad**:
- "The API is broken. What should we do?"

**Good**:
- "The API is broken. I've evaluated 3 alternatives. My recommendation is Vendor B ($50K, 1-week integration). Can I proceed?"

**Your boss wants**:
- To know about problems early
- To see you've thought it through
- To make decisions, not solve problems for you

**5. Make Them Look Good**

**How**:
- Credit them in meetings ("As Sarah suggested...")
- Prepare them for exec presentations
- Handle fires before they escalate
- Make their life easier

**Example - Facebook PM**:
Mark Zuckerberg asked a PM in a meeting: "What's the status of feature X?"

**Option A** (Bad):
PM: "Uh, let me check..." (Mark annoyed)

**Option B** (Good):
PM's manager: "We're on track for Q2 launch. I'll send you the full update after this meeting." (Mark satisfied)

**Behind the scenes**: PM sent their manager a weekly update email that manager forwarded to Mark's EA for CEO briefing doc.

**Result**: PM made manager look informed, manager made Mark feel in control.

## Influence Strategies / Chiáº¿n LÆ°á»£c áº¢nh HÆ°á»Ÿng

**English:**

### The Six Principles of Influence (Cialdini)

**1. Reciprocity**
- **Principle**: People feel obligated to give back
- **Application**: Help stakeholders first, ask later
- **Example**: Help Sales close a deal â†’ They support your feature request

**2. Commitment & Consistency**
- **Principle**: People want to be consistent with past actions
- **Application**: Get small "yes" first, build to big "yes"
- **Example**:
  - Week 1: "Do you agree we should improve conversion?" (Yes)
  - Week 2: "Should we test this hypothesis?" (Yes)
  - Week 3: "Can we allocate budget to build winner?" (Yes - they've committed)

**3. Social Proof**
- **Principle**: People follow others' lead
- **Application**: "Other teams are doing this"
- **Example**: "Google, Netflix, and Stripe all use this approach. Our competitor just launched it."

**4. Authority**
- **Principle**: People defer to experts
- **Application**: Leverage data, research, credentials
- **Example**: "According to Gartner research..." or "Our data scientist analyzed..."

**5. Liking**
- **Principle**: People say yes to those they like
- **Application**: Build genuine relationships
- **Example**: Coffee chats, learn about their goals, find common ground

**6. Scarcity**
- **Principle**: People want what's limited
- **Application**: Create urgency
- **Example**: "Competitor is launching in 3 months" or "Market window closes Q4"

## Common Mistakes / Sai Láº§m ThÆ°á»ng Gáº·p

### Mistake 1: One-Size-Fits-All Communication
**Problem**: Same update to CEO and IC engineer

**Why It Fails**:
- CEO wants business impact
- Engineer wants technical details
- Both feel message isn't relevant

**Solution**: Tailor message to audience. CEO gets exec summary, engineer gets technical spec.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Low-Power Stakeholders
**Problem**: Only manage VPs, ignore ICs

**Why It Fails**:
- ICs do the actual work
- They can slow-walk projects
- They become future leaders

**Solution**: Build relationships across levels.

### Mistake 3: Surprising Leadership
**Problem**: Bad news comes up in exec meeting

**Why It Fails**:
- Boss looks uninformed
- Trust erodes
- You lose autonomy

**Solution**: Weekly email updates. Flag risks early.

### Mistake 4: Not Mapping Stakeholders
**Problem**: "I'll just figure it out as I go"

**Why It Fails**:
- Miss key stakeholders
- Wrong communication strategy
- Waste time on wrong people

**Solution**: Spend 30 minutes upfront mapping. Update quarterly.

### Mistake 5: Trying to Please Everyone
**Problem**: Say "yes" to all stakeholder requests

**Why It Fails**:
- Scope creep
- Nothing ships
- Lose focus

**Solution**: Use stakeholder map to prioritize. Say "no" to low-power stakeholders tactfully.

### Mistake 6: No Follow-Through
**Problem**: Promise updates, then go silent

**Why It Fails**:
- Stakeholders feel ignored
- Trust broken
- They become blockers

**Solution**: Calendar recurring updates. Set reminders.
